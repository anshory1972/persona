06

What actually explains the answer

Beyond bid and age, three other patterns show up in the data and the reasoning text behind each answer.

–

Age: no effect at n=100

Younger half 78.4% yes vs. older half 79.6% yes — a 1.2pp gap, not significant (p=0.657). This does not replicate the sharp age split (27 vs. 40 years) seen in the earlier n=20 pilot on this same scenario — at n=20 that gap was very likely sampling noise.

✓

Income is the dominant driver

Respondents who said "Ya" have on average Rp 2,654,644/month per-capita expenditure, vs. Rp 757,125 for "Tidak" — a 3.5× gap. Every persona's reasoning text explicitly weighs the bid against income; this is the clearest, most consistent driver in the whole sample.

~

Gender and education gaps track income, not identity

Perempuan respondents said yes more often than Laki-laki (83.6% vs 73.3%), and Sarjana/D4/S1 respondents far more than other tiers (95.5% vs 72–76% elsewhere) — both descriptive only (not tested in the regression above), and both plausibly proxy for the same underlying income effect rather than an independent gender or credential effect.

07

Adding more covariates

A naive model with all six covariates (bid, age, income, gender, education, household size) fails to converge — income and bid together predict the answer almost perfectly, which is itself a finding worth reporting rather than a fitting nuisance.

What happened Fitting Y ~ Bid + Age + log(kapita) + Gender + Education + Household size hits a singular Hessian — statsmodels reports that 57% of observations are perfectly predicted by that combination. This is quasi-separation: the synthetic respondent doesn't trade off bid against income the way a noisy real-world sample would — it appears to apply something close to a hard affordability-ratio rule (roughly, "yes if the bid is a small enough slice of my monthly income"), which a plain additive logit can't represent without blowing up.

Re-expressing bid relative to each respondent's own income — bid ÷ per-capita expenditure — resolves this. The model below adds age, gender, education, and household size back in on top of that ratio.

✓

The ratio, not any single raw variable, is what actually predicts the answer

log(bid ÷ kapita) alone explains far more of the variation (pseudo R² 0.47) than bid alone did (0.04) or bid+age together (0.04). Once it's in the model, age, gender, education, and household size all drop to non-significant (p > 0.1) — they were never independent drivers, they were noisy proxies for income that the ratio already captures directly.

~

Read this as a property of the simulator, not of Bandung shoppers

Real CVM respondents are noisier than this — two people at the identical bid-to-income ratio don't always give the same answer. An affordability-ratio pseudo R² this high (and the near-complete-separation in the full model) suggests the LLM is applying a fairly consistent internal threshold rather than genuine preference heterogeneity. Useful for pressure-testing a survey instrument; not a substitute for the noise a real field sample would show.

08

Reading this correctly

  • All 100 respondents are synthetic — Claude (Haiku) role-playing SUSENAS-grounded personas, not real survey fieldwork. Treat this as a pilot-design check (does the instrument produce sensible, income-sensitive, non-degenerate answers?), not as a fieldable WTP estimate.
  • The Rp 4,330 mean WTP is an extrapolation beyond the highest bid actually tested (Rp 3,000), and the bootstrap 95% CI is wide (Rp 2,984–13,794) — a real fielded version of this study should add at least one higher bid level (e.g. Rp 5,000–10,000) to pin the curve down where it actually crosses 50%.
  • The 2,000→3,000 non-monotonicity (64% → 76%) is within sampling noise at n=25 per bid cell; it is not read here as a real reversal in preference.
